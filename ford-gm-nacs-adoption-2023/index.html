Ford and GM Ditched Their Charging Standard in 2023. Here's Why It Mattered.

In 2023, Ford and GM shocked the EV world by adopting Tesla's NACS plug. James Carr explains what it meant for drivers and the charging landscape.

JC
James Carr May 13, 2023 · 6 min read

In May and June of 2023, Ford and then General Motors announced they'd be dropping the Combined Charging System connector and adopting Tesla's North American Charging Standard. Not adapting to it. Not adding it as an option. Switching. That's not a minor spec change. That's an admission that the industry got the infrastructure piece badly wrong for the better part of a decade, and that Tesla got it right while everyone else was arguing about plug shapes at standards committee meetings.

I was at a Ford dealer in late May, walking through a Mustang Mach-E delivery with a customer who'd just gotten burned by a dead CCS charger on I-95 outside of Baltimore. She'd sat in a Wawa parking lot for two hours waiting for an Electrify America technician who never showed. When I pulled up the NACS news on my phone and showed her, her first response was, 'So I've been driving around with the wrong plug for two years?' That question stuck with me. Because yes, in a way, she had been.

The significance here goes beyond connector geometry. Ford's announcement on May 25, followed by GM's on June 8, set off a chain reaction across the industry. By the end of 2023, Rivian, Volvo, Polestar, and Mercedes had all pledged to follow. The SAE officially standardized NACS as J3400 in June. What Tesla had been calling a proprietary standard for years was suddenly the de facto industry standard, and it happened faster than anyone except maybe Elon Musk had predicted. Whether that outcome is good for competition is a separate and genuinely complicated question. But for you, as someone who owns or is thinking about buying a non-Tesla EV, it changes the math on almost every charging decision you'll face over the next five years.

How CCS Lost and Why the Industry Let It Happen

The CCS connector, specifically the SAE J1772 combo variant used across North America, was never technically inferior to Tesla's design in any way that should have mattered. It handles up to 350 kW at stations like Electrify America's newest hardware. On paper, it's capable. The problem was never the plug. It was the network behind it.

J.D. Power's 2023 U.S. Electric Vehicle Experience Public Charging Study found that 21 percent of EV drivers who attempted to use a public charging station couldn't complete a charge, the highest failure rate since J.D. Power began tracking it. That number is staggering. One in five attempts failing. Tesla Superchargers, by contrast, had an uptime rate that independent monitoring groups repeatedly put above 99 percent. The hardware reliability gap wasn't close, and drivers felt it every single time they pulled up to an Electrify America or EVgo station and played the broken-screen lottery.

Ford's EV team knew this. They'd been watching customer satisfaction scores for Mach-E and F-150 Lightning owners crater not because the vehicles were bad but because the charging experience was so inconsistent. Switching to NACS gave them a credible answer to a problem they genuinely couldn't fix themselves. GM's situation was more complicated because they'd sunk enormous marketing energy into Ultium and the Ultium Charge 360 network partnership, but the numbers told the same story.

What the NACS Switch Actually Means for Buyers in 2023

Here's the practical timeline you need to understand. No Ford or GM vehicle shipping in 2023 has a NACS port. The Mach-E, the Lightning, the Blazer EV, the Silverado EV. All CCS. Ford said its first NACS-equipped vehicles would arrive in model year 2025, with adapter availability for existing owners starting sometime in 2024. GM gave a similar window. So if you buy a Lightning today, you're on CCS for at least another year before an adapter lets you tap Superchargers.

That adapter matters more than it might sound. Tesla opened its Supercharger network to non-Tesla EVs starting in late 2022 in Europe and rolled out U.S. pilot access through 2023. By mid-2023, Tesla's own data showed over 12,000 Supercharger stalls in North America available to non-Tesla vehicles via the Tesla app. CCS drivers could access those, but only at compatible stalls, which weren't universal across all Supercharger locations yet. NACS native access will change that equation entirely. Every stall, no fumbling with app magic, just plug in the same way a Model Y owner does.

The honest caveat here is cost. Supercharger pricing for non-Tesla owners runs higher than for Tesla subscribers, and Ford's BlueOval Charge Network pricing is genuinely competitive at peak hours. You'll want to run the actual per-kWh numbers for your region before assuming Supercharger access is automatically a better deal. It often is for reliability. It isn't always for price.

The Ripple Effect on Charging Infrastructure Investment

Here's where this gets interesting for the long game. The U.S. federal government's $7.5 billion NEVI program for EV charging infrastructure had already been rolling out through 2023, and NEVI requires CCS compatibility at funded stations. That requirement was written before the NACS dominoes started falling. The Biden administration moved relatively quickly to update guidance, and by late 2023 the NEVI rules were revised to accept J3400 (NACS) as a compliant connector standard.

The Federal Highway Administration's NEVI program is putting serious money into corridor charging, and having one connector standard instead of two actually simplifies the infrastructure math for station operators. Fewer stall types, simpler cable management, one less failure mode. I've talked to two charging station operators in the Southeast who told me independently that the NACS convergence is the single best thing that happened to their business planning in 2023 because it let them stop hedging on hardware procurement.

The less-discussed angle is what this does to the used EV market. A 2022 or 2023 CCS-only Mach-E or Blazer EV will need that adapter to stay fully competitive at charging stops five years from now. Adapters will exist, Ford has committed to that, but it's still a dongle you need to remember and a potential failure point in a chain that already has too many failure points. I'd factor that into resale value projections, especially if you're buying a 2023 model new right now. The depreciation hit from being a CCS-generation vehicle probably isn't catastrophic, but it's real. The NACS adapter rollout in 2024 confirmed that the transition could actually work cleanly for existing owners, but getting to that point required more coordination than most people expected.

What This Tells Us About How EV Standards Actually Get Set

The common take on the Ford-GM NACS switch is that it proves Tesla won the format war. That's true. But the more instructive lesson is about how auto industry standards actually move. The SAE, the international standards bodies, the OEM coalitions. They all endorsed CCS. They built testing infrastructure around it. They wrote federal regulations mandating it. And then one company with a better-maintained private network made all of that institutional momentum irrelevant in the span of about three weeks of announcements.

I'm not entirely sure what to make of that, honestly. My friend Mike runs a small fleet of electric delivery vans in Phoenix, all CCS, and when I told him about the NACS switch his first reaction wasn't relief, it was anxiety, because now he's staring at a fleet transition cost he didn't budget for. Fleet buyers are in a genuinely uncomfortable spot here. They specced vehicles and charging infrastructure around CCS, they signed multi-year service contracts, and now the reference standard is shifting under them. The adapters will help, but fleet charging management systems often care deeply about connector type for scheduling and billing. It's not a disaster, but it's a mess nobody's writing enough about.

The deeper question is whether NACS dominance is good for the consumer long-term. Tesla holds the physical and network infrastructure advantage now in a way that should at minimum make antitrust observers pay attention. Tesla's trajectory since the Model Y launch in 2020 has been building toward exactly this kind of infrastructure control, and that was visible to anyone watching closely. Competition in charging is genuinely good for pricing and reliability. A world where Supercharger access is the primary deciding factor in which EV you buy is a world where Tesla has a structural sales advantage that has nothing to do with the vehicle itself. That deserves more scrutiny than it's getting in the trade press right now.

For you as a buyer in the second half of 2023, the practical position I'd take is this: if you're buying a non-Tesla EV today, ask your dealer specifically about the NACS adapter timeline and get it in writing. Ford has been reasonably specific. GM has been vaguer. Nail down the date and the cost before you sign. The charging experience over the next two years is going to be better because of what happened this summer. But only if the adapter ships when promised, and in this industry, promised timelines are a starting point for negotiation, not a guarantee.

Before you finalize any EV purchase this fall, pull up PlugShare and map every charging stop on your two most common long-distance routes. Count how many are CCS-only versus Tesla Superchargers. If Superchargers dominate your corridor and you're buying a Ford or GM EV, ask the dealer to put the NACS adapter availability date and any associated cost in your purchase agreement. That one step will save you a lot of frustration in 2024 and protect the resale story on a vehicle you're probably planning to own for at least five years.

Frequently asked questions

Can I still use a CCS EV after Ford and GM switch to NACS?

Yes. Ford and GM have both committed to providing NACS adapters for owners of existing CCS vehicles. The adapters allow access to Tesla Superchargers and NACS-native stations. CCS infrastructure isn't disappearing, either. Electrify America and EVgo stations will continue operating with CCS hardware for years.

Will 2023 Ford or GM EVs lose value because they have CCS instead of NACS?

There's likely some resale impact, but it's hard to quantify yet. An adapter solves the functional problem, but buyers five years from now will notice that a 2023 Lightning isn't natively NACS. For most owners the real-world difference will be minor. For high-mileage road-trip users, it matters more.

When will Ford and GM start selling NACS-equipped EVs?

Both automakers have targeted model year 2025 as the start of native NACS hardware on new vehicles. That means early 2024 production starts for some models. Exact timing varies by vehicle line, so check directly with the brand for the specific model you're considering.
