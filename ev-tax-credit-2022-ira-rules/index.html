The New EV Tax Credit Just Broke Most of the Cars It Was Meant to Help

The 2022 EV tax credit overhaul from the Inflation Reduction Act changed the $7,500 rules overnight. Here's who still qualifies and who got shut out.

JC
James Carr July 10, 2022 · 5 min read

If you walked into a dealership this September expecting $7,500 off a new electric car, there's a decent chance you no longer qualify. The Inflation Reduction Act got signed on August 16, and one clause inside it, the final-assembly-in-North-America rule, took effect the same day. No grace period. No transition window. Just a hard line drawn through the middle of the EV market.

I spent that first week after signing on the phone with a buyer in Ohio who'd put a deposit down on a Hyundai Ioniq 5 back in July. Great car. I've driven it, the 800-volt architecture is legitimately good. But it's built in Korea, so the day he went to finalize, the credit he'd budgeted around was simply gone. He asked me if I was sure. I read him the Department of Energy list twice. It was a rough phone call.

So here's my read: this law will help the EV market long term, and it will hurt a lot of specific buyers right now. Both things are true.

The old credit was simple. Buy a qualifying plug-in, get up to $7,500, and the only real catch was a per-manufacturer cap of 200,000 vehicles that had already knocked Tesla and GM out. The new version scraps that cap, which is good news, then bolts on a stack of sourcing, price, and income requirements that are far harder to meet. In trying to reward American manufacturing, Congress wrote a credit that most of the cars people actually want to buy can't claim yet.

What the law actually rewrites

The headline number stays the same. It's the plumbing underneath that changed. The $7,500 is now split into two halves you have to earn separately. Half of it, $3,750, depends on a percentage of the battery's critical minerals being extracted or processed in the U.S. or a free-trade partner. The other $3,750 depends on a percentage of the battery components being manufactured or assembled in North America. Miss both thresholds and you get nothing. Hit one, you get $3,750.

Those percentages ramp up every year, starting at 40 percent for minerals and 50 percent for components in 2023 and climbing toward 80 and 100. There's also a hard exclusion coming for any battery containing materials from a foreign entity of concern, which in plain English is aimed squarely at China. Given that China dominates battery-grade lithium and graphite processing today, that's a wall the industry has to build supply chains around fast.

And then there's the rule that bit first: final assembly must happen in North America. That one had no phase-in. According to the Department of Energy Alternative Fuels Data Center, the list of models meeting even that single requirement dropped from more than 70 to roughly 20 the moment the President signed.

The overnight eligibility cliff

I've covered a lot of policy changes in this beat, and I can't remember one that redrew a shopping list this fast. Cars that qualified on August 15 did not qualify on August 16. That's not a rounding error. That's your Kia EV6, your Ioniq 5, your Toyota bZ4X, your Subaru Solterra, all built overseas, all off the table for the assembly credit until those companies move production here.

What survived, at least for the assembly test, skews toward Detroit and Tesla. The Ford F-150 Lightning, the Chevy Bolt, the Tesla Model Y and Model 3, the VW ID.4 built in Chattanooga. Reuters reported that the group of manufacturers immediately meeting the new terms was small and getting smaller as the tighter battery rules approach in 2023.

Here's the part buyers keep missing. Meeting final assembly does not mean you get the full $7,500. It means you cleared the front door. The battery-sourcing tests in 2023 are where a lot of these North American cars will lose one or both halves, because the mineral supply chains just aren't localized yet. So the 20-car list you see today is going to fracture again in January.

  • Bought before August 16 with a written binding contract? You can likely still claim under the old rules. Keep that paperwork.
  • Buying now? Check the exact VIN's build location, not the model name. Same badge can be built on two continents.
  • Waiting for 2023? Expect the qualifying full-credit list to get shorter before it gets longer.

Price caps, income caps, and who gets shut out

Even if your car clears assembly and battery sourcing, two more gates decide whether you personally can claim it. There's an MSRP cap: $55,000 for cars, $80,000 for SUVs, trucks, and vans. And there's an income cap: $150,000 for single filers, $300,000 for joint. Go over either one and the credit evaporates regardless of the car.

I actually think the income caps are the most defensible piece of this whole law. A federal EV subsidy was, for years, disproportionately flowing to households buying $90,000 luxury vehicles they'd have bought anyway. A Treasury Department analysis of the earlier program made that skew pretty clear. Means-testing a tax dollar isn't a scandal.

The MSRP caps are where it gets messy. Eighty grand sounds generous until you price a well-optioned Lightning or Rivian, both of which blow past it in the trims people actually order. And the car-versus-SUV classification is going to spawn a thousand arguments, because the IRS definition of a crossover doesn't always match what the manufacturer prints on the window sticker. I'd bet money we see cars reclassified to duck under the $80,000 SUV ceiling within a year.

The bet Congress is making, and whether it pays off

Strip away the frustration and here's the actual logic. The old credit subsidized demand and let the supply chain sit wherever it was cheapest, which meant China. This one subsidizes demand only if you build the supply chain here. It's industrial policy wearing a consumer-rebate costume, and it's aimed at a real vulnerability. When roughly 5.8 percent of new U.S. vehicles sold in 2022 were electric, per Cox Automotive, and nearly all the battery cells came from overseas, that's a national supply risk, not just a shopping inconvenience.

Honestly, I went back and forth on this one for a while, and I'm still not totally settled. A buddy of mine who runs an independent shop outside Cleveland texted me the week the law passed asking whether he should even bother stocking chargers for the import brands his customers drive, and I gave him this long confident answer about supply chains reshoring by 2025, and then I hung up and thought, wait, do I actually believe that timeline or did I just read it in a press release. The truth is nobody knows how fast a lithium refinery gets built in Nevada. I've been wrong on manufacturing timelines before. Ask me again in three years.

What I'll commit to is this. The transition pain is real and it lands hardest on people who did nothing wrong except want a Korean EV. But the direction is defensible. If you want to understand why domestic scale matters this much, look back at how the Model Y launch in 2020 turned into a line in the sand for the whole industry. Volume built here is what makes the rest of this work.

Before you sign anything this fall, do one thing: pull the actual VIN of the car you want and check its build plant against the Department of Energy eligibility list, then run your income and the exact MSRP against the caps. Model name alone tells you nothing now. A Volkswagen ID.4 built in Chattanooga qualifies while one built in Germany doesn't, same badge, different answer. Five minutes of checking can be the difference between a $7,500 credit and a $0 surprise at the finance desk.

Frequently asked questions

Does the new EV tax credit still give you $7,500?

It can, but the amount is now split into two $3,750 halves earned separately. One half depends on where the battery's critical minerals come from, the other on where the battery components are made. Many cars will only qualify for half, or none, once the 2023 sourcing rules kick in.

Why did my EV suddenly stop qualifying in August 2022?

The final-assembly-in-North-America requirement took effect the day the Inflation Reduction Act was signed, August 16, with no phase-in. Any EV built outside North America, including popular Korean and Japanese models, lost eligibility immediately regardless of price.

What are the income and price limits for the credit?

The credit caps out at $55,000 MSRP for cars and $80,000 for SUVs, trucks, and vans. Income limits are $150,000 for single filers and $300,000 for joint filers. Exceed either the vehicle price cap or your income cap and you get nothing.
